AfCFTA and the New Africa Trade Corridors:
What Exporters Need to Know in 2026

FP
FinePulse Editorial Team
Logistics Insights, FinePulse
7 min read

For years, the single biggest obstacle preventing African businesses from trading freely with each other wasn't tariffs or politics — it was logistics. A Nigerian manufacturer trying to reach a buyer in Nairobi often found it cheaper and faster to ship to Europe than to a country on the same continent. The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) was built to fix exactly this problem, and in 2026, the practical, on-the-ground changes are starting to genuinely show up — not just as policy announcements, but as real freight cost reductions and new shipping routes that exporters can actually use today.

What Actually Changed: The Numbers Behind the Headlines

Nigeria has expanded its AfCFTA-linked air cargo corridor through a partnership with RwandAir, opening new export routes connecting Nigeria to East and Southern African markets including Kigali, Lusaka, and Harare, alongside additional carrier capacity on existing routes to Nairobi and Johannesburg. For exporters holding AfCFTA Certificates of Origin issued through the Nigeria Customs Service, cargo rates on these RwandAir-operated routes have been set below $2 per kilogram — a dramatic reduction from previous freight charges that had ranged from roughly $3 to as much as $10 per kilogram on some intra-African routes.

This isn't a marginal adjustment. It represents a reduction of over 50% in freight cost for eligible exporters on these specific routes, and the corridor reportedly recorded a 40% increase in export volume during its first year of operation — concrete evidence that lower logistics costs translate directly into more businesses actually using the route, not just a policy success on paper. Looking ahead, plans for a direct cargo route between Gwangju, China and Enugu International Airport, expected to begin in late 2026, signal that this corridor expansion strategy is continuing rather than being treated as a one-time initiative.

Why Intra-African Trade Has Lagged for So Long

Understanding why this matters requires understanding just how underdeveloped intra-African logistics has historically been relative to Africa's trade with the rest of the world. Africa's air freight volume represents only a small single-digit percentage of global air cargo by weight, even though the continent's population and consumer market continue growing rapidly. Within that already-modest volume, trade between African countries — as opposed to trade between Africa and Europe, Asia, or North America — has represented a strikingly small share of total flows, despite considerable underlying demand. A parcel moving between two African cities has frequently had to route through a hub in the Middle East or Europe, extending both transit time and cost in a way that simply doesn't happen for equivalent intra-European or intra-Asian shipments.

The structural reasons are straightforward: limited direct air routes between African nations, historically higher per-kilometer freight costs on underdeveloped routes, and a patchwork of differing customs and regulatory regimes that made cross-border trade administratively harder than shipping to a single destination market outside the continent entirely. AfCFTA exists specifically to dismantle these barriers — tariff reduction is the headline policy, but the logistics corridor development happening alongside it is, in practical terms, equally important to whether the agreement actually translates into more trade.

What Exporters Need to Do to Actually Benefit

The preferential rates and streamlined access AfCFTA offers aren't automatic — they require specific documentation, and missing this step means paying standard rates regardless of how favorable the underlying trade agreement is.

Obtain an AfCFTA Certificate of Origin. This is issued by the Nigeria Customs Service (or the equivalent customs authority in other AfCFTA member states) and certifies that your goods qualify for preferential treatment under the agreement. Without it, you're shipping at standard commercial rates even if your goods would otherwise qualify.

Understand which products and sectors are positioned to benefit most. Current corridor development has particularly targeted agribusiness, fashion and textiles, cosmetics, processed foods, and light manufacturing — sectors identified as having strong export potential once logistics costs come down. If your business operates in one of these categories, the current moment represents a genuinely favorable window to explore new African markets you may have previously written off as logistically impractical.

Engage with the agencies coordinating this work. The corridor expansion has involved coordinated work between the Nigeria Customs Service, the Nigerian Export Promotion Council, and the Federal Airports Authority of Nigeria, among others. Exporters who engage proactively with these agencies — rather than waiting for information to filter down — tend to access new opportunities earlier than those who don't.

The Continuing Logistics Challenges (Because They Haven't Disappeared)

It's worth being honest that AfCFTA's logistics improvements, while genuinely significant, haven't eliminated every structural challenge facing intra-African trade. Direct flight connections between many African city pairs remain limited outside the specific corridors that have received dedicated investment, meaning a route to Nairobi or Lusaka might now be excellent while a route to a different African capital remains roundabout and expensive. Infrastructure investment — airport cargo handling capacity, cold storage for perishables, customs processing technology — varies enormously by country, and a smooth experience exporting through one African nation's customs system doesn't guarantee an equally smooth experience importing into another. And differing regulatory standards between AfCFTA member states, even with the trade agreement in place, still require exporters to understand destination-specific requirements rather than assuming a single compliance approach works continent-wide.

Why This Matters Beyond Nigeria

While the specific developments discussed here center on Nigeria's corridor expansion, the broader trend is continental. Established cargo hubs — Addis Ababa, Nairobi, Johannesburg, Lagos, and Cairo — are each investing in expanded freighter capacity, cold storage infrastructure, and broader route networks, driven by the recognition that Africa's growing middle class and urbanizing population represent a genuinely underdeveloped logistics opportunity relative to the continent's economic trajectory. Africa's air freight volume has been growing at a rate meaningfully above the global average in recent years, even from a smaller base, suggesting the current moment is closer to the beginning of this expansion than its peak.

For exporters anywhere — not just in Nigeria — paying attention to which intra-African corridors are receiving this kind of dedicated investment is a genuinely useful signal for where new market access is becoming practically achievable, as opposed to theoretically possible but logistically impractical.

Practical Advice for Businesses Considering Intra-African Export

Start by identifying whether your product category falls into one of the sectors currently receiving the most corridor investment — agribusiness, textiles and fashion, cosmetics, processed foods, and light manufacturing are clearly prioritized right now, and businesses in adjacent categories may find it worth inquiring whether similar treatment is available or planned.

Secure your AfCFTA Certificate of Origin before you need it, not after a buyer in another African market has already expressed interest — having this documentation ready removes friction at the exact moment you have a real opportunity in front of you.

Research the specific corridor serving your target market rather than assuming general "intra-African shipping" conditions apply uniformly. The RwandAir-Nigeria corridor's pricing and route structure, for example, is specific to that partnership and that set of destinations — a different target market may be served by entirely different infrastructure with different cost and timeline characteristics.

And work with a logistics partner who's actively tracking these developments, since corridor expansions, new partnerships, and updated certificate requirements are happening on a rolling basis rather than as a single completed policy change — staying current matters more on this topic than on most other shipping considerations.

The Bottom Line

AfCFTA's promise has always been straightforward in concept — make it easier for African businesses to trade with each other — but for years the logistics infrastructure simply didn't exist to make that promise practical. What's changing in 2026 is that dedicated corridor investment, like the Nigeria-RwandAir partnership, is starting to convert that policy promise into freight rates and routes businesses can actually use today. For exporters willing to do the documentation work and pay attention to where this investment is concentrated, intra-African trade is shifting from a theoretical opportunity to a genuinely practical one — likely for the first time in a generation.
